Hi everyone.

I'm analyzing ERP data (128 electrodes) with lmer in R and would appreciate
your feedback on my modeling strategy. My experiment involves testing the
interaction between two experimental factors (Factor1 and Factor2). Based
on previous studies, I?m expecting a frontally distributed effect for
Factor 1 and a posteriorly distributed effect for Factor 2, and this is
also clear from the scalp maps. However, I would also justify the selection
of the ROIs using a full-factorial model with topographical factors
(Anteriority [AnteriorVsPosterior] and Laterality [LeftVsCentralVsRight]).
I'm aware of two schools of thought here:

   - *Aggregate electrode* data within each ROI to reduce electrode-level
   variance
   - *Keep individual electrodes* and model electrode-level variance via
   random effects, to preserve statistical power

The topographic factor is a ?nuisance variable?. In other terms is
something that influences the outcome of the model but is not of direct
interest to me: I have to include it in the random structure, not because
it is part of my hypothesis, but because omitting it would lead to
unexpected variability, inflating my p-values, and distorting my estimates.
Basically, I ran into two main problems:

   - *Without* topographic factors, degrees of freedom were *inflated*,
   matching the number of observations, ~300k (the same happened with
   aggregated data)
   - *With* topographic factors in the random structure, however, the
model *failed
   to converge* At this stage, since my main goal is to understand the
   spatial distribution of effects rather than test all interactions
   simultaneously, I considered including a nested formula in the fixed
   effects:

EEG ~ Anteriority * Laterality / Factor1 * Factor2 + (1 + Factor1 * Factor2
| Subject) + (1 + Factor1 * Factor2 | Item) + (1 | Channel)

By doing this, df seems to be under control: 60~80 df (also for 3-way
interactions). The idea behind this approach is not to treat the
topographic analysis as a confirmatory test of the main effects of Factor1
and Factor2. This model serves as a descriptive and exploratory step whose
sole purpose is to characterize the spatial distribution of the effects
across the scalp and to empirically justify the selection of ROIs. Is the
nested formula the right tool for this exploratory stage? Since I?m using
the data to guide ROI selection and then testing the same effects within
those ROIs, should I be sensitive to the issue of circularity (Kriegeskorte
et al., 2009)?


Thanks in advance for any thoughts!

The course is intended for researchers and graduate students with prior experience in regression models in R who want to extend their skills to mixed models and more advanced modelling frameworks.

Day 2: Random effects, variance structures, model selection, and causal vs predictive approaches

Day 4: Spatial, temporal, and phylogenetic correlation structures in GLMMs
The course will combine lectures, discussions, and hands-on exercises via Zoom and Slack.
